## Post 01 — Recovery

### Rest Days Are Part of the Training

You don't get fitter during your workout. You get fitter after it — while you sleep, recover and let your body do its thing.

That's what your Recovery Score is tracking. When it's low, your body is still rebuilding. Pushing hard anyway doesn't make you tougher. It just slows everything down.

On low-recovery days, go for a walk. Do some stretching. Cook a good meal. The gym will still be there tomorrow — and you'll actually get something out of it.

## Post 02 — Sleep

### It's Not Just How Long You Sleep. It's How Well.

You can spend eight hours in bed and still wake up exhausted. Sound familiar?

Sleep quality matters as much as sleep length. The deep sleep you get in the first half of the night is when your body repairs itself. The REM sleep in the second half is when your brain catches up. Cut your night short and you're shortchanging both.

A few things that reliably wreck sleep quality: alcohol, late-night screen time and inconsistent bedtimes. Even a 90-minute difference between your weekday and weekend schedule can throw off your whole week.

FitByte tracks all six sleep stages so you can see exactly what's happening — not just how long you were out.

## Post 03 — GPS Accuracy

### Why Your Old Watch Got the Distance Wrong

Ever finish a run and notice the distance feels off? There's a reason for that.

Most watches use a single GPS signal. In cities, that signal bounces off buildings. In forests, trees block it. The result is a route that drifts — sometimes by hundreds of meters over a 10K.

FitByte Pro and Ultra use two GPS signals that check each other in real time. The result is a track that actually follows where you ran, not where the satellites guessed you were.

Small difference on paper. Big difference when you're chasing a personal best.

## Post 04 — Habits

### Missing One Day Won't Undo Everything

You broke your streak. It happens.

Here's what didn't break: the fitness you built, the sleep patterns you established, the habits you made part of your routine. One missed day doesn't erase any of that.

Streaks are useful because they make consistency feel real and visible. But they're just a tool — not the goal. The goal is showing up most days, for a long time.

So if today was a rest day, fine. Tomorrow is just another day to move.

## Post 05 — Stress & HRV

### Your Body Knows You're Stressed Before Your Brain Does

Heart rate variability — HRV — sounds complicated. It's really just a measure of how recovered your nervous system is.

When you're well-rested and low on stress, your heart rate has a natural rhythm with small variations between each beat. When you're run down, anxious or coming down with something, that variability flattens out.

FitByte measures your HRV while you sleep and flags when it drops below your personal baseline. Not as an alarm — just a heads-up. Something is taking more out of your system than usual.

It might be a big deadline. A bad night. A cold starting to brew. Whatever it is, now you know early enough to do something about it.

## Post 06 — Winter Training

### The Case for Running in the Cold

Dark at 5pm. Damp pavement. Kit that's cold before you even put it on.

Go anyway.

Winter is when fitness gaps open up. Most people slow down or stop entirely. The ones who keep going — even at a lower intensity, even for shorter sessions — come out of spring with a head start.

Cold air is actually easier to run in than summer heat. Your heart doesn't have to work as hard to keep you cool. You might even enjoy it once you're moving.

Dress in light layers, warm up inside first and give yourself permission to go slower. Your Body Battery will tell you if it's a hard-effort day or an easy-jog day.

Either way, out beats in.

## Post 07 — Training Load

### How to Train Hard Without Breaking Down

Most injuries don't happen in a single session. They build up over weeks of quietly doing a bit too much.

Training Load tracks the cumulative stress of all your workouts — runs, rides, gym sessions, everything — and compares it to your recent average. That comparison tells you whether you're in a productive training zone or whether you're piling on faster than your body can adapt.

Ramp up too quickly after a rest week, a trip or an illness and the risk spikes. FitByte flags it before it becomes a problem, not after.

The goal isn't to train less. It's to train consistently — and keep showing up.

## Post 08 — Getting Back to It

### Starting Over Is Still Starting

You took a break. Maybe a few weeks, maybe longer. The first workout back felt nothing like it used to.

That's completely normal. Fitness fades, and rebuilding it takes time. But here's what's also true: it comes back faster than it arrived the first time.

Within two weeks of consistent movement, your resting heart rate starts dropping. Your sleep improves. Your energy levels stabilize. FitByte tracks all of it — so even when progress feels invisible, you can see it happening.

Set your baseline from today. Don't compare it to before.

Three weeks from now you'll be glad you started.

## Post 09 — Understanding Your Data

### The Only Five Numbers You Need to Check

FitByte tracks a lot. Most of it doesn't need daily attention. These five do.

**Recovery Score** — check before you plan your workout. Low score means go easy.

**Resting Heart Rate** — your long-term fitness signal. Watch the trend week over week, not day to day.

**HRV Trend** — one low reading is normal. A downward trend over five or more days means something's off.

**Sleep Score** — aim for above 75. Consistently below 60? Check your bedtime first — consistency matters more than length.

**Training Load** — look at the 28-day view once a week. Are you in the green zone? Creeping into the red?

Five numbers. Five minutes a week. That's all it takes to stay on top of your health without obsessing over it.
